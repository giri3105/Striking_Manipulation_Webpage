01 · Overview

Abstract

While traditional robotic manipulation relies on secure grasping, this approach often struggles with objects that are awkwardly shaped, sized, or positioned. Non-prehensile actions like striking offer a faster, more flexible alternative, but they are notoriously difficult to control due to unpredictable environmental factors like surface friction. To bridge this gap, this project introduces a novel, multi-functional robotic end-effector that seamlessly unifies prehensile (pick-and-place) and non-prehensile (targeted striking) capabilities. At the core of this hardware is a Latch Mediated Spring Actuation (LaMSA) mechanism, which allows the system to efficiently store and release elastic energy for powerful, highly controlled strikes.

Because striking outcomes depend heavily on the physical properties of the object and environment, this project also introduces a hybrid learning and model-based control framework to guide the hardware. Inspired by Rapid Motor Adaptation (RMA), the system uses brief, exploratory interactions to infer the unknown physical characteristics of a target object on the fly. This creates an adaptive, end-to-end framework that minimizes the need for precise prior modeling, allowing the robot to robustly and accurately manipulate objects even when their dynamics are completely unknown.

02 · Motivation

Why Striking Manipulation?

Standard robotic manipulation is inherently limited: objects must be within reach, graspable, and their physical properties must be known. Striking — a non-prehensile primitive — bypasses all three constraints by applying impulsive forces to reposition objects beyond the robot's workspace.

Fig 1. A mobile manipulator transporting a non-graspable object to an unreachable goal location using a combination of motion primitives. At step (5), the robot strikes the object across the remaining distance.Figure adapted from [1]

If striking is the solution for out-of-reach objects, why can't the manipulator just "punch" the object itself? Why build a dedicated striking gripper?

Targeted striking via whole-body manipulation of a 7-DoF arm
Fig 2. Targeted striking with Whole Body Manipulation. The arm uses its full kinematic chain as a striking tool — energy-inefficient and limited by joint torque and velocity caps. Adapted from [2]

Limitations of Whole-Body Striking

Energy Inefficient

Joints must overcome their own inertia to build impact velocity

Workspace Limited

Objects outside the reachable envelope cannot be manipulated

Joint Constrained

Maximum achievable impact velocity is capped by joint speed limits

A dedicated LaMSA mechanism in the end-effector decouples energy generation from joint kinematics — delivering ~44 W of peak striking power from a 7–8 W motor by slowly storing then rapidly releasing elastic energy.

03 · Hardware

Mechanical Design

The end-effector integrates two manipulation modes in one compact mechanism. Pick-and-place uses a passive finger and one Dynamixel servo — mirroring conventional grippers. Striking employs a motor-driven snail cam to gradually compress a spring; a linear actuator unlatches at a precisely timed cam angle θ, converting stored elastic energy into a high-speed impulsive strike.

Fig 3. Working Principle of the LaMSA Striker — the snail cam loads the compression spring (k = 2.2 N/mm) from ℓ₁ to ℓ₂ storing energy E = ½k(ℓ₂−ℓ₁)². On unlatch at cam angle θ, the striker is released over stroke ℓₛ producing impact velocity vₛ = ℓₛ√(k/m).
Key Equations · LaMSA

The actuator (7–8 W) slowly charges the spring. On unlatch, the stored energy is released in ~10 ms — producing ~44 W of instantaneous striking power. This power amplification is the core advantage of LaMSA over direct actuation.

k = 2.2 N/mm  ·  max compression = 12 mm  ·  stroke = 12 mm  ·  total mass ≈ 500 g

E = ½k(ℓ₂ − ℓ₁)² = 0.44 J
vₛ = ℓₛ√(k/m)
J = m·vₛ ≈ 0.0204 kg·m/s
P = E/t ≈ 44 W (t ≈ 10 ms)

04 · System Modelling

System Modelling & Control

Due to latch-mediated energy release and nonlinear contact dynamics, directly measuring striker velocity is challenging. A pendulum-based system identification pipeline estimates velocity indirectly — by matching the pendulum's post-impact oscillation to a calibrated physics model.

Attempts at Direct Velocity Measurement
Vision-Based (ArUco Marker)

❌ Requires high-FPS camera (>500 fps) — not available in the lab setup

Linear Potentiometer

❌ Noise spikes corrupt the time-derivative, yielding unreliable velocity estimates during the rapid strike event

Pendulum Experimental Setup

A simple pendulum (ball + rigid link + rotary encoder) is struck by the end-effector. The resulting angular trajectory θ(t) is recorded and fitted to a physics model to back-compute the striker's initial velocity.

Striker hitting the pendulum ball — post-impact oscillation used for velocity estimation
Fig 6. Pendulum-based system identification experiment. The striker impacts the pendulum ball (0.75× slow-motion) and the oscillation data θ(t) is fed into the identification pipeline.

Two-Phase Identification Pipeline

I

Parameter Identification

Minimize residual between experimental angle θₑ(t) and ODE-simulated output θₘ(t). Recovers effective pendulum length Leff, viscous damping cv, and Coulomb friction cc.

II

Strike Velocity Estimation

With parameters fixed, back-compute the initial angular velocity θ̇₀ imparted by the strike by matching post-impact oscillation. Linear velocity: v₀ = Leff · θ̇₀.

III

Polynomial Velocity Model

A multivariable cubic polynomial maps (d, θ) → vₛ across all experiments. Accuracy: R² = 0.91, RMSE = 0.058 m/s, Bias = 0 m/s.

IDENTIFIED PARAMETERS
Leff = 0.2997 m    cv = 0.0004    cc = 0.1222

Learning-Based Inverse Control

The inverse problem: given desired displacement pg = [x, y], predict control parameters u = [xo, yo, zo, θ].

A Random Forest Regressor (150 trees, max depth 8, 85/15 split) trained on 108 physical strike trials approximates this nonlinear inverse mapping. It is robust to limited data and noisy real-world measurements.

Dataset: 108 physical trials  ·  Split: 85/15
Input: desired object displacement (x, y)
Output: striker offsets (xo, yo, zo) + cam angle θ

06 · Future Work

Future Scope

Environment & Object Independent Adaptation

The current Random Forest model is trained on a specific object and surface, making it brittle to changes. Integrating Rapid Motor Adaptation (RMA) will allow the system to infer object and surface properties online from a short history of early strike interactions, enabling real-time transfer to unseen environments without retraining the base policy.

Application-Oriented Deployment

Deploy in practical settings including dynamic waste-sorting pipelines (non-graspable debris) and skill-based tasks such as robotic carrom playing — a precision multi-step striking domain requiring accurate directional control of object trajectories.
